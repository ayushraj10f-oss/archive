Karmabhumi: Indra’s Curse, Civilizational Stagnation, and rerum indicarum natura

By Ayush Raj

Until the age of Samudragupta, India was a prosperous, martial, and distinctly patrician society. This was because the land itself held the favour of the Vedic gods (Indra, Varuna, Agni, and others) whose blessings were secured through reverence and sacrifice, especially through yajnas.

During the Gupta Empire, particularly at its peak, the Indian state - comprising a capable bureaucracy, disciplined armed forces, and effective secular enforcement mechanisms - was strong enough that the ruling class no longer needed to invoke divine authority to maintain societal order, hierarchy, and civility. The gods were respected, but the state itself upheld the civilizational framework.

After Samudragupta, however, the empire shrivelled and eventually collapsed. Without a centralized or even competently localized ruling class, the power of societal maintenance passed from the state to the priesthood. Yet the priests lacked arms, organized force, and logistical capacity to enforce virtue, discipline, or hierarchy through material means.

Stripped of coercive power, they turned to fiction, constructing mythologies around the gods and performing rituals that depicted supposedly virtuous but ultimately menial and non-divine acts, attempting to impose order through narrative rather than force. These efforts materialized as the Puranas, written by localized, sectarian priesthoods who elevated their preferred deities (mainly Vishnu, Shiva, and Shakti). By then, the worship of the Vedic gods had already been relegated to a martial and aristocratic pursuit, disconnected from the broader populace.

In their desperation, the priests made what may have seemed like a practical decision but was in fact a civilizational blunder: they assimilated random folk and tribal demigods into the broader canon, granting them parity with the 33 principal Vedic deities. This fractured the divine hierarchy and led to the degradation of the Vedic gods, most notably Indra, who faced particular diminishment - mocked, subordinated, and reduced in stature in these sectarian tales.

In response, the noble and violent king of the gods, Indra, cursed the land itself - the geography, not the people. India was condemned as a Karmabhoomi: a land of endless, fruitless toil, where all effort and initiative would ultimately yield nothing enduring. This was karmic justice for the betrayal of the Vedic cosmic order.

At the time the curse was imposed, a significant portion of the population was still self-aware, capable, and even respectable by civilizational standards. Indra, however, chose not to curse the people directly. Instead, he sabotaged the environment itself, ensuring that the land would systematically thwart the ambitions of the capable and self-conscious, condemning them to repeated failure and frustration. The result was that every generation of such individuals would either fail, leave, become cynical extractors, or be crushed by the futility of their efforts.

Over time, this structural curse would tilt the demographic balance. As the competent failed to uplift the land and saw their ambitions decay, the oblivious, the incapable, and the base would by default become the supermajority, not by divine favour but by the entropic force of civilizational decay. Their lack of higher-order thinking, their physical and intellectual mediocrity, and their obliviousness ensured that they did not even perceive the curse; their existence was cursed in itself, though they lacked the consciousness to feel its sting.

Moreover, because the overwhelming majority of Indians remained either unaware of the curse or fundamentally indifferent to resurgence, any effort by the aware few to lead a national revival could never gain momentum. The masses neither desired nor understood such projects, preferring inertia and subsistence over sacrifice and struggle.

This is why India repeatedly comes perilously close to resurgence only to collapse before fruition (the Chahamanas nearly defeating the Ghaznavids and Ghurids, Akbar almost Hinduizing the Mughal elite, Madhavrao and Nana Fadnavis nearly expelling the British and setting the stage for industrialization, affirmative action almost being phased out before the Mandal resurgence). Every ascent is devoured by the land’s inherent curse.

The Indian diaspora confirms this diagnosis. Descendants of upper and middle caste non-noble indentured servants from the backward Gangetic plains have successfully built quasi-first world, high-trust, liveable societies in Mauritius, Guyana, and Trinidad - with Mauritius being the richest country in Africa and Guyana the richest in South America (per capita). The same people, on Indian soil, remain trapped in dysfunction, proving that the curse is geographical, not genetic.

Even the physical degradation of the people was not instituted; Indians who emigrated before the Green Revolution often have descendants with better, more patrician, and distinctly Aryan physiognomies compared to those in the mainland. Likewise, second-generation Indian immigrants in the first world are often as tall or taller than their parents - and sometimes even taller than the host populations.

Yet within the cursed geography of India, all potential, intellectual, civilizational, even physical, remains stunted, devoured by the land's metaphysical affliction.

Merely performing yajnas is not enough. Until the Indian elite internalize and violently enforce the Vedic ethos of war, conquest, and aristocratic exceptionalism, rejecting all delusions of egalitarianism, the curse cannot be broken. Only through the uncompromising restoration of the Vedic sacral order, grounded in sacred violence, hierarchy, and the sovereign right to dominate, can Indra’s favour be regained and the land finally redeemed.
